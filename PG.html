Modern PG (Point Guard): Roles, Responsibilities and Famous Players.

The point guard (PG) is one of the five positions in a regulation basketball game. A point guard has perhaps the most specialized role of any position. Point guards are expected to run the team's offense by controlling the ball and making sure that it gets to the right player at the right time. Above all, the point guard must totally understand and accept their coach's game plan. They must also be able to adapt to what the defense is allowing, and they also must control the pace of the game. This article will introduce about roles of a variety PG models, in order to help you have clearer look about what position you play in.

1. Playmaking Point Guards.

Playmaker PG is the classic style of PGs, playing football right with the mission that this position was born: Control the game and create opportunities. Players of this type focus on the task of coordinating the ball for their teammates and deciding which offensive tactics to use during each attack. They are always the players who lead the assists statistics of the team because they always focus on passing the most favorable passes to the team to score. For players of this type, their priority is always "pass-first", which means they always prefer to pass the ball over to finish. They also do not need good finishing ability but can also shoot long range when the opportunity appears.

In recent years, with the player leading a game of a team that can come from many different positions on the field, the classic PG is gradually becoming rarer. The outstanding Playmaker Point Guard currently playing in the NBA are only a few players like Chis Paul, Raja Rondo, Ricky Rubio ... Of which only Chris Paul is really reaching the level of a superstar.

Chris Paul, one of the most talented Playmaking PGs of all time.

2. Shooting Point Guards.

This is the type of PG is dominating in the NBA today with the appearance of superstars like Steph Curry, Damian Lillard, Kyrie Irving or James Harden. They have one thing in common: They are long-range and medium-range shooters, combined with breakthrough balls that trouble the opponent’s defense. They still have the task of controlling the tempo or creating for the teammates, but their top task is still to score. The arrival of Step Curry and the famous small-ball squad of the Golden State Warriors opened the trend for the use of the Shooting Point Guard. The man who molded a terrifying Golden State and gave Curry a new role was a Sharpshooter Point Guard in the '90s, coach Steve Kerr.

However, there are still diversity in the way this type of PG plays. If Curry and Lillard are constantly moving without the ball and waiting for the space to receive the handball, Harden and Irving still prefer to take the ball in their hands and break through with the jump or pass the ball to the teammates in more vacant position. Before the current generation of Shooting PG, NBA also had the appearance of some powerful Shooting PGs. These include Curry’s coach Steve Kerr or former superstar Steve Nash.

Stephen Curry, controversially the second best PG of all time, is the most terrifying 3pt Shooter of all time.

3. Slashing Point Guards.

Slashing Point Guard is also a new form of point guard appeared in about 10 years in the NBA. This is a model of muscular guard who has superior fitness to other PGs. They are also the main attackers of the team because they can easily overcome other guards with bodybuilding as well as their strength. This PG model has the ability to score diversely. They prefer to break into the paint area with their speed and strength, which can kill or draw the opponent on their own and create a favorable position outside the 3pt arc for shooters. To deal with these types of PGs, opponents often have to use players in the SG or SF position to guard or use the 2-to-1 strategy (double team). This is also a rare PG model because not all players acquire the sufficient bodybuilding and power to play with this role.

The outstanding Slashing Point Guard players are all NBA stars, including Derrick Rose, John Wall or Russell Westbrook. Especially in the 2016-2017 season, by making Giannis Antetokounmpo to play in the PG position, Milwaukee Bucks coach Jason Kidd set an unprecedented scenario when using a 2m11 tall player with a 2m21 armspan to play in this position.

Back in his time in Chicago Bulls, Derrick Rose, NBA youngest MVP of all time, is a typical Slashing PG.

4. Defensive Point Guards.

This is an extremely rare form of dribbling defender in previous and current NBA history. The defender of this type has the ability to defend extremely well. They have the main task of stopping opponent’s stars from scoring. These PGs often possess great physical strength as well as the ability to concentrate highly during the game. On the offensive front, they usually only have simple passes to players who have the ability to create or score better.

Sometimes they also finish themselves at the opportunity, however their main task is still defense. They are also the start of the home team's counterattack thanks to their ability to steal the ball very quickly. The most famous defensive PG in history is obviously Gary "The Glove" Payton. In NBA today there are still some defensive PG models such as Patrick Berverley or Dante Exum.

Gary Payton is the most famous defensive PG in the NBA history
